Who Said What — Reference Attribution

Who introduced the original LLaMEA framework? ▼
van Stein and Back (2024) introduced LLaMEA (Large Language Model Evolutionary Algorithm), which established a feedback-driven cycle where an LLM iteratively refines algorithms based on runtime error logs and performance metrics. It originally used a (1+1) Evolution Strategy to keep computational costs low.
van Stein, N. & Back, T. (2024). LLaMEA: A Large Language Model Evolutionary Algorithm for Automatically Generating Metaheuristics.
Who proposed Evolution of Heuristics (EoH)? ▼
Liu et al. (2024) introduced EoH, a dual-population strategy that evolves both the Python code and the natural-language "thought" associated with it. It creates a semantic bridge ensuring that code changes are driven by conceptual reasoning rather than random token flipping. EoH includes exploration prompt strategies (e1, e2) that combine multiple parent heuristics at the conceptual level.
Liu, F., Tong, X., Yuan, M., & Zhang, Q. (2024). Evolution of Heuristics: Towards Efficient Automatic Algorithm Design Using Large Language Model.
Who developed LLaMEA-BO and what was its key contribution? ▼
Li et al. (2025) developed LLaMEA-BO for automatically generating Bayesian Optimization algorithms. Their key contribution was demonstrating that transitioning from mutation-only (1+1) to population-based evolution with explicit crossover operators significantly improved performance. They showed BO algorithms are particularly suited for semantic recombination because their components (surrogate models, acquisition functions, initializers) are highly modular and composable. Their TREvol solver, which emerged from a crossover operation, outperformed state-of-the-art baselines.
Li, X., van Stein, N., & Back, T. (2025). LLaMEA-BO: Evolving BO with LLMs.
Who proposed Discounted Thompson Sampling (D-TS)? ▼
Raj and Kalyani (2017) originally proposed Discounted Thompson Sampling, a variant that applies an exponential decay factor (gamma) to posterior parameters, reducing the influence of outdated observations while maintaining stable mean estimates. Qi et al. (2023) then demonstrated its effectiveness in evolutionary algorithm operator selection, showing that properly tuning the discount factor governs the effective memory length of the system, condensing the complex problem of adaptive control into a single hyperparameter.
Raj, V. & Kalyani, S. (2017). Taming Non-stationary Bandits: A Bayesian Approach.
Qi, Y., Li, K., & Gong, W. (2023). D-TS for Adaptive Operator Selection.
Who introduced CodeEvolve (CODEEVOLVE)? ▼
Assumpcao et al. (2026) introduced CodeEvolve, which operationalizes inspiration-based crossover where the LLM explicitly integrates patterns, logic, and functional components from multiple parent solutions during generation. Their ablation showed that configurations using inspiration-based crossover (with 2 or more parent solutions) outperform strong baselines in general algorithmic discovery, while depth-only configurations without crossover do not.
Assumpcao, G., et al. (2026). CodeEvolve: LLM-driven Evolutionary Framework.
Who introduced LMX (Language Model Crossover)? ▼
Meyerson et al. (2024) introduced LMX, the first LLM crossover operator not designed for a specific domain. Rather than prompting the LLM to combine solutions explicitly, LMX concatenates parent solutions as few-shot examples and generates offspring through pattern completion: the LLM infers the shared structure and continues it. Using a 6B-parameter CodeGen model in the Sodaracer robot domain, it produced roughly 30% valid offspring. However, for optimization metaheuristics where correctness requires consistent logic across the whole program, this pattern-matching approach may fall short.
Meyerson, E., et al. (2024). Language Model Crossover: Variation through Few-Shot Prompting.
Who established the Building Block Hypothesis? ▼
Goldberg (1989) articulated the Building Block Hypothesis, extending Holland's Schema Theorem (1975). The hypothesis argues that complex optimal solutions emerge through the hierarchical combination of shorter, low-order, highly fit subsets of the genome ("schemata" or "building blocks"). This is the theoretical foundation for why crossover is essential — it enables the direct recombination of these semantic components, whereas mutation alone would require a precise sequence of random modifications to reconstruct one feature within another.
Goldberg, D. E. (1989). Genetic Algorithms in Search, Optimization, and Machine Learning. Addison-Wesley.
Who provided runtime analysis proving crossover superiority over mutation? ▼
Opris et al. (2025) conducted rigorous runtime analysis demonstrating that on many objective landscapes, crossover-enabled algorithms achieve polynomial-time convergence, whereas mutation-only baselines require exponential time. This theoretical gap highlights a critical deficiency in current LLM-based optimizers, which remain predominantly mutation-centric.
Opris, A., et al. (2025). Runtime Analysis of Crossover vs Mutation-Only EAs.
Who introduced the No Free Lunch theorems and why are they relevant? ▼
Wolpert and Macready (1997) formulated the NFL theorems, stating that averaged over all possible problem classes, no single optimization algorithm outperforms any other. This justifies the entire field of Automated Algorithm Design — the goal is not to find a "universal" heuristic but to develop generative frameworks that can adapt to the problem at hand. Zhang et al. (2025) frame this in their new taxonomy of "Meta-Black-Box-Optimization" (MetaBBO), arguing the future is not in better static algorithms but in systems that generate algorithms on the fly.
Wolpert, D. & Macready, W. (1997). No Free Lunch Theorems for Optimization.
Zhang, Z., et al. (2025). MetaBBO Taxonomy.
Who proposed the MA-BBOB benchmark? ▼
Vermetten et al. (2023) proposed MA-BBOB (Many-Affine Black-Box Optimization Benchmark), which extends the classical BBOB suite by generating optimization landscapes as affine combinations of existing benchmark functions. This creates more realistic and diverse test scenarios. The study used the BLADE evaluation framework by van Stein et al. (2025) for standardized evaluation protocols.
Vermetten, D., et al. (2023). MA-BBOB: A Benchmark Suite with Known Optima.
van Stein, N., et al. (2025). BLADE: Benchmarking LLM-driven Algorithm Discovery and Evolution.
Who introduced the Elo rating system used in evaluation? ▼
Albers and de Vries (2001) developed the Elo rating system adapted for algorithm comparison. Algorithms are compared in tournament-style one-against-one matches across benchmark instances and random seeds, providing a single scalar measure of competitive strength. This approach is well-established in competitive learning systems; notably, Silver et al. (2018) used Elo-based evaluation in AlphaZero.
Albers, S. & de Vries, S. (2001). Elo Rating System for Algorithm Comparison.
Who proposed the Exploration-Exploitation Dilemma that underpins this work? ▼
March (1991) originally formulated the Exploration-Exploitation Dilemma, later adapted to evolutionary systems by Holland (1992). An effective optimizer must balance exploration (acquiring new information about the search space) and exploitation (refining known high-quality solutions). Static algorithms typically fail to manage this trade-off effectively. Yin et al. (2024) further reinforced this with empirical evidence showing static parameter configurations are insufficient in LLM-driven systems.
March, J. G. (1991). Exploration and Exploitation in Organizational Learning.
Who introduced FunSearch? ▼
Romera-Paredes et al. (2024), published in Nature. FunSearch (Function Search) pairs an LLM with a systematic evaluator to solve hard combinatorial problems like the Cap Set problem. It uses a "best-so-far" prompting strategy, effectively performing local hill-climbing search in the space of Python programs.
Romera-Paredes, B., et al. (2024). Mathematical Discoveries from Program Search with Large Language Models. Nature.
Who introduced Eureka? ▼
Ma et al. (2024) introduced Eureka in the domain of Reinforcement Learning. It uses GPT-4 to write reward functions for robotic control and introduces an "evolutionary reflection" mechanism where the LLM analyzes failure logs to generate improved code. Eureka outperforms expert human-designed rewards on 83% of benchmark tasks.
Ma, Y., et al. (2024). Eureka: Human-Level Reward Design via Coding Large Language Models.
Who proposed Adaptive Operator Selection (AOS) with extreme value methods? ▼
Fialho et al. (2008) proposed Extreme Value Based Adaptive Operator Selection, establishing AOS as a standard approach in evolutionary computation for dynamically allocating variation operators during search. Their work is foundational to the bandit-based operator selection mechanism used in GA-LLaMEA.
Fialho, A., Da Costa, L., Schoenauer, M., & Sebag, M. (2008). Extreme Value Based Adaptive Operator Selection. PPSN X.
Who introduced MCTS-AHD? ▼
Zheng et al. (2025) proposed MCTS-AHD, which applies Upper Confidence Bound for Trees (UCT) to select between six LLM-based operators: two crossover actions (e1, e2), two mutation actions (m1, m2), one initialization action (i1), and one tree-path reasoning action (s1). The exploration coefficient in UCT decays linearly, making it a time-scheduled policy — unlike D-TS which responds to actual observed rewards.
Zheng, S., et al. (2025). MCTS-AHD: Monte Carlo Tree Search for Automated Heuristic Design.
Who developed the behaviour-space analysis methodology? ▼
van Stein et al. (2025) developed the behaviour-space analysis methodology used in this study, including the 11 behavioural metrics, the gold-standard performance profile concept, and the feature-selection protocol using Pearson correlation with a redundancy threshold of |r| >= 0.7. The same study established the BLADE benchmarking framework.
van Stein, N., et al. (2025). BLADE: Benchmarking LLM-driven Algorithm Discovery and Evolution.
Who introduced Code Evolution Graphs (CEGs)? ▼
van Stein et al. (2025a/2026) introduced Code Evolution Graphs, which are constructed based on static code features extracted from generated heuristics. CEGs enable direct analysis of how algorithms evolve over successive generations, linking structural properties of code with performance outcomes. They also introduced the SAGE paradigm, which uses CEG-derived structural features in the reward signal.
van Stein, N., et al. (2025a). Code Evolution Graphs for LLM-driven AAD.
van Stein, N., et al. (2026). SAGE: Structure-Aware Graph Evolution.

Why This Approach — Methodology Justifications

Why use Discounted Thompson Sampling instead of UCB or standard Thompson Sampling? Critical ▼
Three reasons:
  • Non-stationarity: Optimization is inherently non-stationary — the effectiveness of operators changes over time as the search progresses from exploration to exploitation. Classical MAB algorithms like UCB and standard TS assume stationary rewards, an assumption critically violated under concept drift (Cavenaghi et al., 2021).
  • Reward-responsive vs. time-scheduled: UCT in MCTS-AHD (Zheng et al., 2025) uses a linearly decaying exploration coefficient — allocation follows elapsed time, not observed rewards. D-TS maintains per-operator Beta distributions updated after every evaluation, so selection responds to actual reward history.
  • Single hyperparameter: D-TS condenses the complex problem of adaptive control into a single hyperparameter: the discount factor gamma (Qi et al., 2023). This governs effective memory length — high gamma = long memory, low gamma = rapid adaptation.
Key defense point: "D-TS was chosen because optimization is non-stationary. UCB assumes stationarity. MCTS-AHD's UCT follows a time schedule, not observed rewards. D-TS adapts to the actual reward landscape."
Why introduce crossover into LLM-driven algorithm design? Critical ▼
  • Theoretical justification (Building Block Hypothesis): Goldberg (1989) argues that complex solutions emerge through hierarchical combination of building blocks. Mutation alone cannot efficiently recombine building blocks from different parents — it would require a precise sequence of random modifications (exponential time per Opris et al., 2025).
  • Empirical evidence from adjacent domains: LLaMEA-BO (Li et al., 2025) showed crossover outperforms mutation-only for Bayesian Optimization. CodeEvolve (Assumpcao et al., 2026) showed inspiration-based crossover with >= 2 parents outperforms depth-only configurations.
  • The gap: No framework had introduced and ablated a general-purpose crossover operator within the LLaMEA paradigm for continuous black-box optimization heuristics. LLaMEA-BO requires BO pipeline modularity. ReEvo's crossover is entangled with its reflective memory. EoH's exploration prompts sit inside a broader five-strategy GA.
Key defense point: "Crossover is theoretically necessary (Building Block Hypothesis), empirically validated in adjacent domains (LLaMEA-BO, CodeEvolve), and specifically absent in the continuous BBO heuristic generation paradigm that we target."
Why use "inspiration-based" crossover rather than syntactic splicing (like LMX)? High ▼
LMX (Meyerson et al., 2024) generates offspring through pattern completion — it concatenates parents as few-shot examples and the LLM infers shared structure. This works for simple programs but for optimization metaheuristics, where correctness requires consistent logic across the whole program, pattern-matching may fall short (only ~30% valid offspring with CodeGen 6B).

Inspiration-based crossover instead prompts the LLM to conceptually analyze multiple parent solutions, extract their strengths (e.g., "selection strategy from Parent A, update rule from Parent B"), and synthesize a new coherent solution. This operates at a semantic level rather than syntactic, producing functionally correct offspring.

Bouras et al. (2025) found that giving the LLM explicit context about parent fitness and code structure consistently outperforms random edit selection in genetic improvement of software.
Why use 3 inspiration sources for crossover instead of 1 or 2? High ▼
The ablation study (Section 4.6.1) directly answers this:
  • 1 inspiration: LLaMEA-Crossover1-NoRefine achieved mean AOCC 0.7994 — a 1.0% decrease below mutation-only baseline (0.8098). With one source, the LLM cannot perform meaningful recombination; it lacks building-block diversity and introduces variability without compensating gains.
  • 3 inspirations: LLaMEA-Crossover3-NoRefine achieved mean AOCC 0.8364 — a 2.7% improvement. Multiple sources provide the diversity needed for the LLM to synthesize richer and more complementary strategies.
Goldberg (1989) establishes that crossover produces benefit only when sufficient building-block diversity exists across parents. Three inspiration sources provide that diversity; one does not.
Why use absolute fitness for reward rather than relative-to-parent? ▼
The reward system uses absolute fitness: r_t = max(0, min(1, f_child)) if valid, 0 if crashed. This differs from relative-to-parent approaches because:
  • Absolute reward prevents the system from rewarding marginal improvements from a weak parent while penalizing strong offspring that don't beat an already-good parent
  • It provides a consistent signal across different operator types (crossover offspring may come from multiple parents, making relative comparison ambiguous)
  • The AOCC fitness is already normalized to [0, 1], making it suitable for direct use as a reward signal
Why add a Stagnation Detection and Forced Override mechanism? ▼
The D-TS controller can lock onto a suboptimal operator preference if one operator accumulates strong early rewards. The stagnation detection mechanism (with threshold s_max = 10 consecutive non-improving evaluations) forces operator group switching — from the best of {refine, simplify} to {crossover, random_new} or vice versa. This prevents the controller from persisting with an exploitation-heavy strategy when the search has plateaued.

This is supported by Rajabi and Witt (2023) and Doerr and Rajabi (2024) who analyzed stagnation detection in evolutionary algorithms.
Why use Gemini-2.5-Flash instead of GPT-4 or Claude? ▼
The study used Gemini-2.5-Flash accessed via the Vertex AI API. The choice was based on:
  • Cost efficiency: With 100 LLM evaluations per run across 5 seeds, plus 3 methods and ablation variants, the total API cost was a significant consideration
  • Speed: Flash variants offer lower latency, important when running hundreds of evaluations
  • Advanced reasoning: Despite being a "flash" variant, Gemini-2.5-Flash provides advanced reasoning capabilities suitable for code generation tasks
Limitation acknowledged: Results were obtained with a single model; broader testing across alternative LLM architectures is recommended (Recommendation 1).
Why use MA-BBOB instead of standard BBOB or real-world benchmarks? ▼
MA-BBOB (Vermetten et al., 2023) extends the classical 24-function BBOB suite by generating affine combinations of benchmark functions, creating more diverse and realistic optimization landscapes. Standard BBOB has been extensively studied and many algorithms may overfit to its specific characteristics. MA-BBOB provides a more challenging test while maintaining the standardized evaluation infrastructure of COCO (Hansen et al., 2021). The 5-dimensional setting was chosen to maintain feasible computational budgets.
Why maintain only 4 parents (mu=4) but initialize with 8? ▼
  • Initialization with 8: The ablation study (Section 4.6.2) showed that GA-LLaMEA-8 (8-parent init) achieved 0.8348 mean AOCC vs. GA-LLaMEA-4's 0.7737 — a 7.9% improvement. Larger initialization enhances early-stage diversity, allowing exploration of a broader space of candidate algorithms.
  • Maintain only 4 (mu=4): After initialization, the top-4 are selected as the maintained population. This (mu+lambda) selection pressure ensures only the best solutions survive while keeping the population small enough for the LLM to receive meaningful context about each parent.
  • Lambda=8 offspring per generation: Each generation produces 8 new candidates, providing sufficient sampling of the operator space while staying within computational budget.
Why 4 operators specifically (Refine, Simplify, Crossover, Random New)? ▼
Each operator serves a distinct role in the exploration-exploitation spectrum:
OperatorRoleE-E Balance
RefineTargeted refinement that increases algorithmic complexity, adding new strategies or mechanismsExploitation
SimplifyReduces complexity while preserving or improving fitness — structural regularizationExploitation
CrossoverCombines complementary building blocks from multiple parents into a new coherent solutionBalanced
Random NewGenerates entirely new algorithms from scratch — maximum diversity injectionExploration
This set covers the full spectrum from pure exploitation (Simplify) to pure exploration (Random New), with Refine and Crossover providing intermediate positions.
Why use In-Context Learning (ICL) rather than fine-tuning the LLM? ▼
The methodology is strictly delimited to ICL and prompt engineering to isolate the efficacy of the evolutionary architecture rather than the model's learning capacity. This is a deliberate scope decision:
  • Fine-tuning would confound the evaluation — improvements could be attributed to the model's learned weights rather than the framework's architectural innovations
  • ICL makes the framework model-agnostic and immediately applicable to any LLM
  • It aligns with the principle that LLMs possess emergent reasoning capabilities that resemble an evolutionary search process (Brown et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022)
Why was LLaMEA-BO excluded from the baselines? ▼
LLaMEA-BO (Li et al., 2025) was excluded because it is domain-specific — designed for Bayesian Optimization pipeline generation, not general continuous black-box optimization. BO algorithms have modular components (surrogate models, acquisition functions, initializers) that make crossover easier. The study focuses on general metaheuristic generation where algorithms are monolithic code, making crossover fundamentally harder.
Why use gamma = 0.99 for the discount factor? ▼
Gamma = 0.99 provides a relatively long memory, meaning the controller adapts gradually rather than reactively. With ~100 evaluations across ~12 generations, the effective memory spans approximately the last 70-80 observations (as old observations decay to <50% weight after ~70 steps). This allows the controller to track genuine performance trends while not overreacting to noisy individual evaluations. Qi et al. (2023) demonstrated that properly tuning this single hyperparameter governs the trade-off between adaptation speed and estimation stability.
Why profile ALL generated algorithms (1,442 total) rather than just the best? ▼
Profiling every algorithm generated during evolution (472 from Baseline LLaMEA, 482 from EoH, 488 from GA-LLaMEA) provides insight into the search dynamics of each method, not just the endpoint. This reveals:
  • How efficiently each method explores vs. exploits
  • The consistency of generated algorithms (Q4 proportions: GA-LLaMEA 36.3% vs. EoH 17.6%)
  • Whether high performance comes from a few lucky draws or systematic search improvement
This follows the methodology of van Stein et al. (2025), who established that characterizing the full population is necessary to distinguish between methods that find good solutions by chance vs. by design.
Why use both AOCC and Elo for evaluation? Don't they measure the same thing? ▼
No — they measure complementary properties:
MetricWhat it measuresScope
EloPairwise win rates among the best algorithms produced at evaluation timePeak competitive quality
Mean AOCCAggregates over all generated algorithms throughout the full evolutionary searchOverall search efficiency
This explains the Elo vs. AOCC ordering discrepancy: Elo ranks GA-LLaMEA > EoH > Baseline, while AOCC ranks GA-LLaMEA > Baseline > EoH. EoH's best algorithms are tournament-competitive (high Elo), but its many low-quality exploratory candidates depress its aggregate mean AOCC.

What do the three behavioural mechanisms explain? ▼
Three mechanisms explain GA-LLaMEA's superior AOCC performance:
  1. Generative crossover enables combining complementary algorithmic strategies from multiple parents (Sudholt, 2014), producing offspring that inherit effective exploitation behaviours. Reflected in high Intensification Ratio (0.8927) and low Distance-to-Best (0.4241).
  2. D-TS controller adaptively balances exploration and exploitation at the operator level. It progressively shifts selection toward Simplify and Crossover operators, which produce algorithms with stronger exploitation profiles. This steers the meta-level search toward the gold standard region.
  3. Late-stage refinement capability: GA-LLaMEA's algorithms sustain improvement later into the evaluation budget (Last Improvement Fraction 0.3501 vs. 0.2677 for LLaMEA and 0.1436 for EoH), indicating more effective late-stage exploitation.
What do the Code Evolution Graphs (CEGs) reveal? ▼
CEGs reveal fundamentally different evolutionary dynamics:
  • EoH: Appears as disconnected point clouds without explicit lineage tracking — no genealogical relationships between generated algorithms. This limits interpretability.
  • Baseline LLaMEA: Shows branching structures but with high variability in token count and irregular structural transitions. Mutation-driven changes in isolation lead to inconsistent refinement and abrupt complexity increases.
  • GA-LLaMEA: Demonstrates more coherent and directed evolutionary trajectories. Connected lineages show smoother transitions across generations, with complexity growth accompanied by performance improvements (constructive, not uncontrolled expansion).
The CEGs provide visual evidence that GA-LLaMEA's recombination and adaptive selection mechanisms promote more stable and interpretable evolution (van Stein et al., 2026).
What do the operator selection dynamics show? ▼
Figure 4.8 shows a clear adaptive pattern transitioning from exploration to exploitation:
  • Early generations: More evenly distributed, with higher Random New selection — reflecting the need for structural diversity and broad exploration
  • Later generations: Simplify and Crossover emerge as dominant strategies, indicating a shift toward intensification and refinement
  • Crossover achieves the highest mean reward (Figure 4.9), validating it as a productive search mechanism
  • Random New consistently has the lowest reward (~0.3-0.4), but is necessary early on for diversity
This transition demonstrates that D-TS successfully adapts to non-stationary reward distributions (Qi et al., 2023).

What does the Elo rating of ~1575 actually mean in practical terms? ▼
The Elo scale is logarithmic. A +140 Elo gap (GA-LLaMEA vs. Baseline LLaMEA) translates to a significant and reliable competitive advantage in pairwise comparisons across diverse MA-BBOB instances. For context, in chess an 85-point gap (GA-LLaMEA vs. EoH) corresponds to an expected win rate of approximately 62%. The lower confidence bound of GA-LLaMEA remains substantially above the mean performance of Baseline LLaMEA.
Why does GA-LLaMEA have the highest Intensification Ratio? ▼
GA-LLaMEA's median Intensification Ratio (0.8927) — tracking the proportion of offspring generated near the current best — is highest because:
  1. Crossover inherits exploitation behaviours from multiple strong parents, producing offspring that search near the best-found solutions
  2. The D-TS controller progressively favours Simplify and Crossover — both exploitation-oriented operators
  3. The per-method correlation between Intensification Ratio and fitness is strongest for GA-LLaMEA (r = 0.667 vs. 0.453 for Baseline, 0.509 for EoH)
What is the significance of the Q4 proportion (36.3%)? ▼
GA-LLaMEA places 177 of its 488 generated algorithms (36.3%) in the top performance quartile (AOCC >= 0.8802), compared to 99/472 (21.0%) for Baseline LLaMEA and 85/482 (17.6%) for EoH. This indicates GA-LLaMEA not only produces the best individual algorithms but generates a substantially higher proportion of high-performing candidates throughout its evolutionary search — demonstrating systematic quality improvement, not just lucky draws.
Why is D-TS described as a "variance-reduction mechanism" rather than a "mean-improvement mechanism"? ▼
Comparing full GA-LLaMEA (mean AOCC 0.8348, sigma 0.0255) against LLaMEA-Crossover3-NoRefine (mean AOCC 0.8364, slightly higher), adding D-TS slightly reduces mean fitness but substantially reduces variance. All 5 runs of GA-LLaMEA exceed 0.80, whereas the fixed three-inspiration variant shows greater variability. This makes D-TS primarily a stabilizer that ensures consistent performance, a distinction consistent with adaptive operator selection theory (Qi et al., 2023).
How many total LLM evaluations were used across all experiments? ▼
Each run uses 100 LLM evaluations, run 5 times per configuration:
  • Main comparison: 3 methods x 5 seeds x 100 evals = 1,500 evals
  • Crossover ablation: 3 configs x 5 seeds x 100 evals = 1,500 evals
  • Population ablation: 2 configs x 5 seeds x 100 evals = 1,000 evals
  • Total: ~4,000 LLM evaluations
Plus initialization evaluations (8 per run = 200 more for main comparison). Each evaluation includes benchmark execution across all 24 MA-BBOB functions in 5D.

Technical Deep-Dive — Equations & Parameters

Walk me through the D-TS update process step by step. Critical ▼
After each offspring evaluation, following Qi et al. (2023) Algorithm 1 with Gaussian posteriors:
  1. Discount ALL arms: For each arm $i$: $N_i \leftarrow \gamma \cdot N_i$, $\tilde{\mu}_i \leftarrow \gamma \cdot \tilde{\mu}_i$. This exponentially decays old observations.
  2. Update selected arm $k$: $N_k \leftarrow N_k + 1$, $\tilde{\mu}_k \leftarrow \tilde{\mu}_k + r_t$ (where $r_t$ is the absolute AOCC reward)
  3. Compute posterior: $\hat{\mu}_k = \tilde{\mu}_k / N_k$ (discounted empirical mean), $\tau_k = \min(1/\sqrt{N_k},\;\tau_{\max})$ (capped std dev)
  4. Sample: For each arm $i$, draw $\theta_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\hat{\mu}_i, \tau_i^2)$ — Gaussian posterior, NOT Beta
  5. Select: Choose arm $k = \arg\max_i \theta_i$ (Thompson Sampling)
  6. Epsilon-greedy floor: With probability $\varepsilon = 0.4$, select uniformly at random instead (prevents arm extinction)
D-TS Posterior Update (Qi et al., Algorithm 1)
$$\hat{\mu}_i = \frac{\tilde{\mu}_t(\gamma, i)}{N_t(\gamma, i)}, \quad \tau_i = \min\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{N_t(\gamma, i)}},\; \tau_{\max}\right)$$ $$\theta_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\hat{\mu}_i,\; \tau_i^2) \quad \text{for each arm } i$$
The discount factor $\gamma = 0.99$ ensures recent observations carry more weight (effective window $\approx 100$ observations), adapting to the non-stationary reward landscape of evolutionary search.

bandit.py — _update_posterior() 
def _update_posterior(self, arm_state: ArmState) -> None:
    count = max(self.epsilon, arm_state.discounted_count)
    # Paper Algorithm 1, line 11: mu_hat = mu~/N
    arm_state.posterior_mean = arm_state.discounted_sum / count
    # Paper Algorithm 1, line 12: tau = min(1/sqrt(N), tau_max)
    arm_state.posterior_var = min(1.0 / count, self.tau_max ** 2)
What is the AOCC formula and how is it computed? ▼
AOCC (Area Over the Convergence Curve) measures anytime performance:
AOCC = (1/n) * sum_{i=1}^{n} [ (log10(y_i) - log10(f*)) / (log10(ub) - log10(lb)) ]

Where: y_i = best fitness at evaluation i, f* = known global optimum, lb/ub = bounds for normalization.
Clipping: min(max(y_i, log10(lb)), log10(ub)) constrains extreme values.
Result: AOCC lies in [0, 1]; higher = faster and more consistent convergence.
The final scalar fitness used for evolutionary selection is obtained by averaging AOCC scores across all benchmark instances and independent runs.

How does the stagnation detection work in Algorithm 3.1? ▼
In the pseudocode (Algorithm 3.1, lines 15-18):
  1. A counter $s$ tracks consecutive evaluations without improvement to the best-so-far
  2. If $f_{\text{child}} > f_{\text{best}}$ (new best found): reset $s = 0$, update best
  3. Otherwise: $s = s + 1$
  4. When $s \geq s_{\max}$ (10): force operator selection from the opposite group — if the bandit's best arm is in {refine, simplify}, switch to {crossover, random_new} and vice versa. Reset $s = 0$.

    self._stagnation_counter = 0

After each generation, the combined pool of mu parents + lambda offspring is ranked by fitness, and the top mu individuals are retained as the new parent population (Algorithm 3.1, line 48: P = top-mu(P)). Why Gaussian posteriors instead of Beta? How does D-TS sampling work? ▼
Important clarification: The implementation uses Gaussian (Normal) posteriors, not Beta distributions. This follows Qi et al. (2023) Algorithm 1 exactly:
  • The posterior mean $\hat{\mu}_i$ is the discounted empirical average of observed rewards
  • The posterior variance $\tau_i^2 = \min(1/N_i, \tau_{\max}^2)$ — simple $1/n$ variance, capped by $\tau_{\max}$
  • $\theta_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\hat{\mu}_i, \tau_i^2)$ — sample from a Gaussian to balance exploration vs exploitation
  • Arms with fewer observations have higher variance → naturally explored more often
  • Arms with many observations converge toward their empirical mean → exploited when good
Why not Beta? Beta is conjugate for binary rewards. Our rewards are continuous AOCC values in $[0,1]$, making Gaussian a better fit. The variance cap $\tau_{\max} = 0.2$ prevents over-exploration of arms with very few observations.

Limitations, Threats & Future Work

What are the main limitations of this study? Critical ▼
  1. Single model, single benchmark: Evaluated exclusively on 5D MA-BBOB with Gemini-2.5-Flash. No evidence of generalization to higher dimensions, different LLMs, or different problem domains.
  2. Binary reward signal: D-TS uses a binary valid/invalid reward. More nuanced feedback (multi-objective, structural quality) could improve controller decisions.
  3. Fixed discount factor: gamma = 0.99 is static throughout the run. A self-adaptive schedule could improve late-stage responsiveness.
  4. Late-stage exploitation gap: Last Improvement Fraction (0.3501) remains below the Q4 gold standard (0.5252), marking late-stage exploitation as the primary remaining weakness.
  5. Prompt sensitivity: Performance profiles shifted across template versions during development. New application domains will require domain-specific prompt adaptation.
  6. No D-TS isolation condition: The ablation design compares crossover variants but does not include a condition with multi-inspiration crossover + uniform operator selection (no D-TS). The independent contribution of D-TS beyond crossover cannot be directly isolated.
  7. No fine-tuning: Limited to ICL — fine-tuned models might perform differently.
  8. Computational cost: 100 LLM API calls per run is non-trivial.
What are the 5 recommendations for future work? ▼
  1. Scalability, Model Architecture, and Prompt Robustness: Test across higher-dimensional spaces, alternative LLMs, and measure prompt sensitivity systematically.
  2. Generative Crossover Design and Inspiration Diversity: Characterize the minimum diversity threshold below which recombination hurts. Systematic study across problem domains and LLM architectures.
  3. Adaptive Controller Refinement: Test multi-objective rewards, self-adaptive discount schedules, and alternative bandit algorithms (UCB variants, EXP3, reinforcement-learning-based controllers).
  4. Structurally-Informed Operator Selection: Feed CEG-derived structural features (lineage connectivity, complexity growth, genealogical stability) into the reward signal, following the SAGE paradigm (van Stein et al., 2026).
  5. Domain Generalization: Extend to combinatorial scheduling, data mining, multi-objective problems, and real-world robotics applications (particularly deformable object manipulation).

Is crossover always beneficial? When does it hurt? ▼
No — crossover is NOT unconditionally beneficial. The ablation study shows single-inspiration crossover (1 parent) actually hurts performance (-1.0%). LHNS (Xie et al., 2025) is worth noting as counter-evidence: single-solution Ruin-and-Recreate heuristic design without crossover can match population-based approaches.

Crossover requires sufficient building-block diversity to be productive (Goldberg, 1989). Below a minimum diversity threshold, recombination replaces focused mutation-based refinement with noisier combinations that degrade solution quality. Three inspiration sources provide that diversity; one does not.

Conceptual / Theoretical Foundations

What are the three structural gaps in current LLM-driven AAD that GA-LLaMEA addresses? Critical ▼
  1. Mutation-centric evolution: Most frameworks (Baseline LLaMEA, FunSearch) rely solely on mutation. Without crossover, candidates evolve in parallel disconnected refinement streams, restricted to local basins of attraction. Opris et al. (2025) prove this leads to exponential convergence time.
  2. Static operator scheduling: EoH applies operators at fixed uniform probability throughout evolution. Without adaptive control, the system cannot shift from exploration to exploitation as the search matures. This creates a persistent exploration bias.
  3. Syntactic crossover (pattern-completion): LMX (Meyerson et al., 2024) generates offspring by continuing a few-shot pattern rather than reasoning about algorithmic structure, with no guarantee the resulting code will be functionally correct.
GA-LLaMEA bridges all three gaps through inspiration-based generative crossover + D-TS adaptive operator control + per-operator normalized reward baselines.
What is the difference between Algorithm Selection and Algorithm Generation? ▼
Zhang et al. (2025) frame this distinction in their MetaBBO taxonomy:
  • Algorithm Selection: Choose the best algorithm from a pre-existing portfolio for a given problem. Limited by the quality and diversity of the portfolio.
  • Algorithm Generation: Create entirely new algorithms on-the-fly, tailored to the problem. No predefined pool — the search space is the space of all possible programs.
GA-LLaMEA operates in the Algorithm Generation paradigm, leveraging LLMs to explore an open-ended program space. The NFL theorems (Wolpert & Macready, 1997) justify this shift — since no universal algorithm exists, the future lies in generating problem-specific solutions.
What are the three eras of Automated Algorithm Design? ▼
Following Tan et al. (2025):
  1. Genetic Programming (Era 1): Koza (1992) — evolves programs as syntax trees. Suffered from code bloat, limited function sets, and poor interpretability.
  2. Hyper-heuristics (Era 2): Burke et al. (2013), Drake et al. (2020) — "heuristics to choose/generate heuristics." Selection hyper-heuristics manage pre-existing operator pools; generation hyper-heuristics use GP to construct new ones. Still suffered from the "Interpretability Gap."
  3. LLM-based Evolution (Era 3): Current era — LLMs bridge automated synthesis and human readability. FunSearch, EoH, LLaMEA represent this paradigm. LLMs can explain the logic behind modifications, solving the interpretability crisis.
What is In-Context Learning and why is it relevant to this work? ▼
In-Context Learning (ICL), introduced by Brown et al. (2020), is the ability of LLMs to learn to perform a task simply by observing examples in the prompt, without weight updates. This is critical because:
  • GA-LLaMEA leverages ICL by providing parent algorithms and performance feedback in the prompt — the LLM learns what works and what doesn't from these examples
  • Chain of Thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022) enables LLMs to decompose complex problems into intermediate steps, solving the interpretability crisis of the GP era
  • The framework explicitly avoids fine-tuning to isolate the evolutionary architecture's contribution
How does GA-LLaMEA relate to the Holland Schema Theorem? ▼
Holland's Schema Theorem (1975) and Goldberg's Building Block Hypothesis (1989) provide the theoretical justification for crossover in GA-LLaMEA:
  • Short, low-order, highly fit subsets ("schemata" or "building blocks") grow exponentially in frequency over generations
  • Crossover enables the direct recombination of building blocks from different parents — e.g., one parent's selection strategy combined with another's update rule
  • Mutation alone is unlikely to merge two distinct features, as it requires a precise sequence of random modifications
  • In the LLM context, this extends to semantic building blocks — the LLM understands algorithmic logic and can combine high-level strategies coherently
LLaMEA-BO (Li et al., 2025) and CodeEvolve (Assumpcao et al., 2026) provide empirical validation of the Building Block Hypothesis in the context of LLM code evolution.
What is a "non-stationary reward distribution" and why does it matter? ▼
In evolutionary search, operator effectiveness changes over time: Random New is useful early (diversity injection) but wasteful late; Simplify/Crossover become more productive as good solutions emerge. This means reward distributions drift — an operator's average reward at generation 1 differs from generation 12.

Classical bandits (UCB, standard TS) assume stationary rewards (Cavenaghi et al., 2021). They accumulate historical rewards without decay, potentially overcommitting to operators that were good early but are suboptimal now. D-TS addresses this by exponentially decaying old observations, enabling faster adaptation to the shifting reward landscape.
What is COCO and how was it used? ▼
COCO (Comparing Continuous Optimizers) is a standardized platform for benchmarking continuous optimization algorithms, developed by Hansen et al. (2021). It provides:
  • 24 BBOB benchmark functions with known optima
  • Standardized evaluation protocols and data logging
  • Automated performance metric computation
In this study, generated Python algorithms must be compatible with the COCO platform. Each algorithm is evaluated across all 24 MA-BBOB function instances in 5D, with AOCC computed from the optimization traces.
What is "deception" in optimization and how does crossover help? ▼
Optimization landscapes often contain deceptive regions that mislead search algorithms toward suboptimal solutions. Mutation-centric approaches are particularly vulnerable as they rely on incremental improvements that reinforce local optima.

Recombination provides a mechanism for escaping deceptive regions by enabling discontinuous jumps in the search space. By combining distinct algorithmic components, crossover facilitates exploration beyond local basins of attraction. This is why GA-LLaMEA's crossover operator specifically selects diverse inspirations from the population — ensuring the combined solution can break free from a single exploitation trajectory.

Show me the discount mechanism. How does exponential forgetting work in code? High ▼
Before each reward update, ALL arms' statistics are multiplied by $\gamma$:

bandit.py — _apply_discount() + update() 
def _apply_discount(self) -> None:
    """Apply exponential discount to all arm statistics."""
    if self.discount == 1.0:
        return  # No discounting
    for arm_state in self.arms.values():
        arm_state.discounted_count *= self.discount
        arm_state.discounted_sum *= self.discount
        arm_state.discounted_sum_sq *= self.discount

def update(self, arm_name: str, reward: float) -> None:
    # Apply discount to ALL arms (exponential forgetting)
    self._apply_discount()
    # Update selected arm with new observation
    arm_state = self.arms[arm_name]
    arm_state.discounted_count += 1.0
    arm_state.discounted_sum += reward
    arm_state.discounted_sum_sq += reward ** 2
    arm_state.pulls += 1
    self._update_posterior(arm_state)
Effect: With $\gamma = 0.99$, an observation from 100 steps ago has weight $0.99^{100} \approx 0.37$ — it contributes about a third of its original influence. This "effective window" of ~100 observations allows the controller to adapt within roughly one full evolutionary run.

How does the (mu + lambda) selection preserve diversity? ▼

def _select(self, combined: List) -> List:
    """(mu+lambda) selection with diversity preservation."""
    # Sort by fitness descending
    sorted_pool = sorted(combined, key=lambda s: s.fitness, reverse=True)
    selected = []

Key Formulas & Equations Reference

Write out the complete D-TS algorithm mathematically. Critical ▼
Discounted Thompson Sampling (Qi et al., 2023 — Algorithm 1)
Initialization: For each arm $i$: $N_0(\gamma, i) = 0$, $\tilde{\mu}_0(\gamma, i) = 0$

At each time step $t$:
1. Discount all arms: $$N_t(\gamma, i) = \gamma \cdot N_{t-1}(\gamma, i) \quad \forall i$$ $$\tilde{\mu}_t(\gamma, i) = \gamma \cdot \tilde{\mu}_{t-1}(\gamma, i) \quad \forall i$$ 2. Compute posterior parameters: $$\hat{\mu}_i = \frac{\tilde{\mu}_t(\gamma, i)}{N_t(\gamma, i)}, \quad \tau_i = \min\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{N_t(\gamma, i)}},\; \tau_{\max}\right)$$ 3. Sample and select: $$\theta_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\hat{\mu}_i, \tau_i^2) \quad \forall i$$ $$a_t = \arg\max_i \theta_i \quad \text{(with probability } 1-\varepsilon\text{)}$$ $$a_t \sim \text{Uniform}(\mathcal{A}) \quad \text{(with probability } \varepsilon\text{)}$$ 4. Observe reward and update: $$N_t(\gamma, a_t) = N_t(\gamma, a_t) + 1$$ $$\tilde{\mu}_t(\gamma, a_t) = \tilde{\mu}_t(\gamma, a_t) + r_t$$
Parameters: $\gamma = 0.99$ (discount), $\tau_{\max} = 0.20$ (variance cap), $\varepsilon = 0.4$ (exploration floor).
What is the AOCC formula in full mathematical notation? High ▼
Area Over the Convergence Curve (AOCC)
$$\text{AOCC} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\log_{10}(y_i) - \log_{10}(f^*)}{\log_{10}(\text{ub}) - \log_{10}(\text{lb})}$$ Where:
  • $y_i$ = best fitness found at evaluation $i$ (clipped to $[\text{lb}, \text{ub}]$)
  • $f^*$ = known global optimum
  • $\text{lb}, \text{ub}$ = normalization bounds
  • $n$ = total evaluations (budget = 10,000)
Result: $\text{AOCC} \in [0, 1]$; higher = faster, more consistent convergence.
The final scalar fitness is the mean AOCC across all 24 MA-BBOB function instances and 5 independent runs per instance.
Write out the reward function formally. ▼
Absolute Fitness Reward (Equation 3.2)
$$r_t = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } v_t = 0 \quad \text{(invalid code: crash, timeout, syntax error)} \\ \text{clip}(f_{\text{child}}, 0, 1) & \text{if } v_t = 1 \quad \text{(valid code)} \end{cases}$$ Where $\text{clip}(x, a, b) = \max(a, \min(b, x))$ and $f_{\text{child}}$ is the AOCC score.
Why absolute (not relative)? Relative reward ($f_{\text{child}} - f_{\text{parent}}$) creates per-operator baseline asymmetry: Simplify receives inflated rewards for trivial improvements over mediocre parents, while Crossover is punished for not beating the best parent. Absolute reward asks: "which operator produces the highest-quality solutions on average?"
What is the effective window of the discount factor? ▼
Discount Factor Effective Window
The weight of an observation from $k$ steps ago is $\gamma^k$: $$w_k = \gamma^k = 0.99^k$$
Half-life: $k_{1/2} = \frac{\ln(0.5)}{\ln(\gamma)} = \frac{-0.693}{-0.01005} \approx 69$ steps

Effective window (1% weight): $k_{0.01} = \frac{\ln(0.01)}{\ln(0.99)} \approx 458$ steps

Practical window ($\approx 37\%$ weight): $k \approx \frac{1}{1-\gamma} = \frac{1}{0.01} = 100$ steps
With a budget of 100 LLM evaluations and $\gamma = 0.99$, the effective window spans essentially the entire run. This is a gentle discount — observations from the start of the run still carry $\sim 37\%$ of their original weight by the end. More aggressive discounting (e.g., $\gamma = 0.9$, window $\approx 10$) would make the controller more reactive but less stable.

What is the (mu + lambda) selection formula? ▼
Elitist Selection Strategy
$$P_{t+1} = \text{top-}\mu\!\left(P_t \cup O_t\right) \quad \text{subject to diversity constraint}$$ Where:
  • $P_t$ = current parent population ($|P_t| = \mu = 4$)
  • $O_t$ = offspring generated this generation ($|O_t| = \lambda = 8$)
  • $\text{top-}\mu$ selects the $\mu$ highest-fitness individuals with unique code
Combined pool: $|P_t \cup O_t| = 12$ candidates compete for $\mu = 4$ survival slots.
Selection pressure: $\lambda / \mu = 8/4 = 2$, meaning each parent must outperform at least one offspring on average to survive. This creates moderate selection pressure — strong enough to maintain quality, gentle enough to allow innovative solutions time to develop.
