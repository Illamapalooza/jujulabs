Discounted Thompson Sampling
1:30

Continuing with our methodology, I will explain the core adaptive mechanism of GA-LLaMEA — Discounted Thompson Sampling, or D-T-S.

The fundamental challenge is that operator effectiveness changes over the course of an evolutionary run. Generative Crossover may be highly productive early when population diversity is high, but less effective once the population has converged. A fixed schedule cannot respond to this shifting landscape.

D-T-S addresses this as a non-stationary multi-armed bandit problem. As you can see in the bar chart, each of our four operators — Refine, Simplify, Crossover, and Random New — is modeled as an arm with its own posterior distribution. The height represents the estimated mean reward, and the shaded region represents uncertainty.

The key parameters are shown on the left: gamma equals 0.99, which gives us an effective memory window of approximately 100 steps — matching our evaluation budget. Tau-max equals 0.2 as a variance ceiling. Epsilon equals 0.15 as an exploration floor. And s-max equals 10 as the stagnation patience threshold.

On the right, the core equations — Equation 3-5 computes the posterior mean, Equation 3-7 draws a Thompson sample from each arm’s Gaussian posterior, and Equation 3-8 selects the arm with the highest sampled value.

D-TS: Statistical Foundation
1:30

Let me now walk through the mathematical foundation. Equations 3-1 and 3-2 define the discounted statistics.

N-t-of-i is the discounted count — how many times operator i has been selected, weighted by recency. S-t-of-i is the discounted cumulative reward. The key is the gamma-to-the-power-of-t-minus-j term — this exponentially down-weights older observations. At gamma equals 0.99, an observation from 100 steps ago receives weight 0.99 to the power of 100, which is approximately 0.37 — still meaningful, but far less influential than a recent observation.

Equations 3-5 and 3-6 derive the posterior parameters. Mu-hat — the posterior mean — is simply the discounted reward divided by the discounted count. Tau-squared — the posterior variance — decreases as we collect more data, making the posterior tighter. The tau-max ceiling of 0.2 prevents unstable sampling for under-explored arms.

The three annotation cards summarize the practical behavior: discounted statistics adapt to non-stationarity, the discount-then-add update runs in O-of-K time per step, and less data means wider posteriors which means more exploration — Thompson Sampling’s natural exploration-exploitation balance.

D-TS: Operator Selection Policy
1:30

This slide presents the complete operator selection policy — Equation 3-13 — which integrates all three control mechanisms in strict priority order.

Case 1, the highest priority, is the stagnation override. If the stagnation counter s reaches s-max equals 10 — meaning 10 consecutive evaluations with no A-O-C-C improvement — the framework overrides normal D-T-S selection and forces a call from the best operator group k-star. This is computed by Equation 3-12, a nested double-max that selects whichever group — exploitation or exploration — has the highest-performing single member based on posterior mean.

Case 2 is the exploration floor. With probability epsilon equals 0.15, an operator is chosen uniformly at random from all four arms, regardless of posterior estimates. This prevents permanent abandonment of any arm due to early bad luck — a well-documented failure mode in constrained bandit settings.

Case 3 is standard Thompson Sampling. With probability 0.85, we draw a sample from each arm’s Gaussian posterior and select the arm with the highest sampled value. This is where the adaptive learning happens — arms with higher estimated means are selected more often, but arms with high uncertainty can still win, providing natural exploration.

These three mechanisms are complementary, not redundant. The epsilon floor provides guaranteed coverage, D-T-S provides adaptive temporal selection, and the stagnation override provides deterministic escape from convergence traps.

Reward System
1:00

The reward function, defined in Equation 3-14, is how the D-T-S controller receives feedback about operator quality.

It is a piecewise function with two cases. If the generated algorithm is invalid — meaning the LLM produced code that crashes or fails to execute — the reward is zero. If the algorithm is valid, the reward equals the A-O-C-C fitness score, clipped to the range zero to one.

A critical design choice is that we use absolute fitness, not relative improvement over the parent. Using improvement over the current best would create reward inflation — as the population approaches the fitness ceiling, even good operators would receive near-zero rewards, corrupting the posterior estimates.

The visual flow at the bottom shows the complete pipeline: the LLM generates an offspring, it is executed on MA-BBOB, and the outcome is either a crash — which yields reward zero — or a valid solution — which yields the clipped A-O-C-C score. This reward then updates the D-T-S posterior for the operator that was used.

Experimental Setup
1:30

For our experimental setup, we follow the BLADE benchmarking protocol by van Stein et al., 2025.

The benchmark is MA-BBOB by Vermetten et al. in 2023 — which constructs benchmark instances by computing affine combinations of classical BBOB functions, placing the global optimum uniformly at random. This prevents LLM-generated algorithms from achieving artificially high performance by exploiting structural regularities.

We use 24 noiseless functions in 5 dimensions, with 100 LLM evaluations per run across 5 independent seeds. The population is configured with mu equals 4 maintained parents and lambda equals 8 offspring, with an initialization size of 8 parents — meaning we generate 8 initial candidates and keep the best 4.

The A-O-C-C formula shown at the top is our primary convergence metric — Equation 3-16. It measures how quickly an algorithm converges toward the global optimum, integrating performance across the entire evaluation budget. The nested min-max clipping ensures numerical stability by constraining log-scaled precision to the range negative 8 to 2.

Our baselines are Baseline LLaMEA — the original framework by van Stein and Bäck — and Evolution of Heuristics by Liu et al.

Evaluation Metrics
1:00

We evaluate our framework using four complementary instruments.

First, A-O-C-C — Area Over the Convergence Curve — our primary anytime performance metric. Higher values in the range zero to one indicate faster and more consistent convergence.

Second, Competitive Elo Ratings — a standardized relative ranking based on pairwise tournament matches across all 120 benchmark instances. While A-O-C-C measures aggregate performance over all algorithms, Elo specifically measures the quality of the best algorithms produced.

Third, Behaviour-Space Analysis — which profiles every single generated algorithm — not just the final best — across 11 metrics covering spatial exploration, exploitation intensity, and convergence stability.

And fourth, Code Evolution Graphs, or CEGs — structural analyses that trace the genealogical lineage and code complexity progression across generations, revealing whether evolution builds constructively or drifts.

Why GA-LLaMEA Outperforms
1:30

So why does GA-LLaMEA outperform? We identify three converging behavioural mechanisms.

First, Generative Crossover enables trait combination. The high intensification ratio and low distance-to-best indicate that crossover successfully combines complementary strategies from multiple parents. This is consistent with Goldberg’s Building Block Hypothesis — effective recombination assembles high-performing sub-strategies into superior composite algorithms. The per-method correlation between intensification ratio and fitness is 0.667.

Second, D-T-S steers the evolutionary search toward the gold standard behavioural region. By progressively shifting operator selection toward Simplify and Crossover — the operators that produce the most gold-standard-like algorithms — the adaptive controller optimizes not just individual algorithm quality but the meta-level search trajectory.

Third, sustained late-stage improvement. GA-LLaMEA’s last improvement fraction of 0.3501 — compared to 0.2677 for Baseline and just 0.1436 for EoH — means the framework continues finding better solutions near the evaluation budget limit. This is a direct consequence of the stagnation override mechanism and the adaptive operator selection working together.

A key observation about EoH — shown in the note box — is that it has the most exploratory profile of all three frameworks. Its e1 and e2 operators prioritize diversity over refinement, creating a persistent exploration bias that limits final performance.
