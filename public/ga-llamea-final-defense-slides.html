Introduction

Background of the Study

1

The Algorithm Design Challenge

Solving real-world optimization problems requires designing metaheuristic algorithms — a process traditionally done manually by domain experts, requiring extensive trial-and-error and deep mathematical intuition (Wolpert & Macready, 1997)

2

LLMs Enable Automated Algorithm Design

Large Language Models can now generate executable optimization code from natural-language prompts, enabling a new paradigm: LLM-driven Automated Algorithm Design (AAD). Frameworks like LLaMEA and EoH treat the LLM as an evolutionary operator that iteratively refines algorithms (van Stein & Bäck, 2024; Liu et al., 2024)

3

Current Limitations Motivate GA-LLaMEA

However, despite these advances, current LLM-driven frameworks still have fundamental architectural limitations that restrict the quality and diversity of generated algorithms. These limitations are what motivated us to develop GA-LLaMEA.

Core insight: By combining generative crossover (multi-parent recombination via LLM reasoning) with Discounted Thompson Sampling (bandit-based adaptive control), GA-LLaMEA addresses the fundamental limitations of current LLM-driven evolutionary frameworks.
Background: optimization requires algorithm design, traditionally manual. LLMs now enable automated algorithm generation. But current frameworks have key limitations: no crossover, no adaptive selection. This motivates GA-LLaMEA.

Problem Statement

#ProblemWhy It Matters
1 Underutilized Populations Frameworks like LLaMEA rely on mutation-based refinement — candidates improved in isolation, preventing recombination of complementary strategies, leading to premature convergence
2 Ineffective Recombination No general-purpose crossover exists for LLaMEA in continuous optimization; syntactic crossover produces invalid or logically inconsistent offspring
3 No Adaptive Selection Multiple operators without adaptive control creates a scheduling challenge — static probabilities cannot respond to shifting exploration/exploitation needs

Bouras et al., 2025; Hemberg & O'Reilly, 2024; Aydin et al., 2023

Three core research problems. First, mutation-centric frameworks underutilize population diversity. Second, existing crossover mechanisms produce syntactically invalid code. Third, there is no adaptive mechanism to select among operators.

  • Excludes combinatorial, LSGO (>100D), and multi-objective
  • Computational budget constraints (API costs + latency)
  • Framework validated on Gemini-2.5-Flash only (no fine-tuning, ICL-only). Multi-model generalization is a future direction.

Literature

Theoretical Foundations

What does current AAD already do?

LLaMEA and EoH generate metaheuristics via iterative LLM prompting with μ+λ selection. However, they remain mutation-centric — candidates evolve in isolation without cross-population information sharing.

van Stein & Bäck, 2024; Liu et al., 2024

Why add an Adaptive Operator Selector?

With 4 operators, static probabilities cannot respond to shifting effectiveness. A bandit controller learns which operator is productive at each stage, eliminating fragile manual scheduling.

Aydin et al., 2024; Fialho et al., 2008; Yin et al., 2024

Why D-TS over UCB or sliding-window?

UCB uses time-scheduled decay — exploration decreases with elapsed evaluations. D-TS uses exponential discounting (γ=0.99), responding to actual reward history rather than a clock.

Qi et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2025; Cavenaghi et al., 2021

Why introduce crossover?

Mutation alone restricts search to local basins (Opris et al., 2025). Crossover exists in LLaMEA-BO and ReEvo, but no prior work introduces general-purpose crossover within LLaMEA for continuous black-box heuristics.

Opris, 2025; Li et al., 2025; Assumpção et al., 2026

Four targeted questions addressing our theoretical foundation. Each maps directly to a research problem and design decision.

Core Equations

\[ \hat{\mu}_t(i) = \frac{S_t(i)}{\max(\epsilon,\, N_t(i))} \quad \text{(3-5)} \] \[ \theta_t(i) \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\hat{\mu}_t(i),\, \tau_t^2(i)\right) \quad \text{(3-7)} \] \[ i_t = \arg\max_{i \in \mathcal{A}} \theta_t(i) \quad \text{(3-8)} \]
Visual explanation of D-TS. Each operator arm has a posterior. Crossover has the highest mean. The bar chart makes the bandit concept intuitive for the panel. Equations 3-5 (posterior mean), 3-7 (Thompson sample), 3-8 (operator selection) formalize the mechanism.
Methodology

D-TS — Statistical Foundation

Discounted statistics per operator i (recency-weighted):

\[ N_t(i) = \textstyle\sum_{j=1}^{t} \gamma^{t-j}\mathbf{1}\{i_j\!=\!i\} \quad S_t(i) = \textstyle\sum_{j=1}^{t} \gamma^{t-j} r_j\mathbf{1}\{i_j\!=\!i\} \tag{3-1,2} \]

Posterior belief from discounted sufficient statistics:

\[ \hat{\mu}_t(i) = \frac{S_t(i)}{\max(\epsilon,N_t(i))} \qquad \tau_t^2(i) = \min\!\bigl\{\tfrac{1}{\max(\epsilon, N_t(i))},\,\tau_{\max}^2\bigr\} \tag{3-5,6} \]
Nt = discounted count, St = discounted reward. γ=0.99 → memory ≈ 100 steps.
Discount-then-add: all arms decay each step, selected arm gains +1 count and +rt reward.
μ̂ = performance signal. τ² = uncertainty (capped at τmax=0.2). Less data → wider exploration.
This slide presents the discounted statistics and posterior estimation that underpin D-TS. Equations 3-1 through 3-6. Recursive update (Eq 3-3,4): N_t(i) ← γN_{t-1}(i), S_t(i) ← γS_{t-1}(i); then N_t(i_t) += 1, S_t(i_t) += r_t. O(K) per step.
Methodology

D-TS — Operator Selection Policy

Sample from each arm’s posterior:

\[ \theta_t(i)\sim\mathcal{N}\!\left(\hat{\mu}_t(i),\,\tau_t^2(i)\right) \tag{3-7} \]

Stagnation-aware selection (3 cases):

\[ i_t = \begin{cases} \text{Uniform}(k^*), & s \geq s_{\max} \\ \text{Uniform}(\mathcal{A}), & \text{prob}\;\epsilon \\ \arg\max_{i}\,\theta_t(i), & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \tag{3-13} \]
Case 1 — Stagnation: s ≥ 10 → switch to best group k*
Case 2 — Exploration: ε=0.15 uniform random
Case 3 — Thompson: highest posterior sample
Complete operator selection policy. Thompson sampling (Eq 3-7), exploration floor (Eq 3-10), and full stagnation-aware 3-case rule (Eq 3-13). k* = argmax over operator groups G_e, G_x of max posterior mean (Eq 3-12). This is the slide the panel will probe most directly.
Methodology

Reward System

Per Equation 3-14 — piecewise reward function:

\[ r_t = \begin{cases} 0, & v = 0 \\ \max\!\bigl(0,\, \min(1,\, f_{\text{child}})\bigr), & v = 1 \end{cases} \quad \text{(Eq 3-14)} \]

v = validity flag (1 if algorithm runs + evaluates, 0 if crash). Absolute fitness clipped to [0,1] — prevents reward inflation near the fitness ceiling.

\[ \hat{\mu}_t(i) \approx \mathbb{E}[f_{\text{child}} \mid \text{operator } i] \quad \text{(Eq 3-15)} \]

Methodology

Evaluation Metrics

■ Area Over the Convergence Curve (AOCC)

An anytime performance metric capturing convergence speed and final solution quality.

\[ \text{AOCC}=\frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B}\frac{\log_{10}(\text{ub})-\min\!\left(\max(y_{i},\,\log_{10}(\text{lb})),\,\log_{10}(\text{ub})\right)}{\log_{10}(\text{ub})-\log_{10}(\text{lb})} \]

Higher values in [0, 1] indicate faster and more consistent convergence.

■ Competitive Elo Ratings

Provides a standardized relative ranking based on tournament-style one-against-one matches across all 120 benchmark instances (24 functions × 5 seeds).

Measures the quality of best algorithms produced, complementing AOCC which measures all algorithms.

■ Behaviour-Space Analysis

Profiles every generated algorithm across 11 metrics:

  • Spatial exploration: Avg Nearest-Neighbors Distance, Coverage Dispersion
  • Exploitation: Intensification Ratio
  • Stability: Longest No-Improvement Streak

■ Code Evolution Graphs (CEGs)

Structural analysis tracing genealogical lineage, algorithmic stability, and code complexity progression across generations.

Reveals whether evolution builds constructively or drifts — a unique diagnostic for LLM-driven code synthesis.

Four core evaluation instruments. AOCC captures anytime convergence performance. Elo provides relative ranking via pairwise tournaments. Behaviour-space profiles all 1,442 generated algorithms across 11 metrics. CEGs trace code lineage and complexity evolution.

Why GA-LLaMEA Outperforms

1

Generative Crossover Enables Trait Combination

High Intensification Ratio (0.8927), low Distance-to-Best (0.4241). Per-method correlation: Inten-Ratio → fitness r = 0.667 (Building Block Hypothesis: Goldberg, 1989; Holland, 1992)

2

D-TS Steers Toward Gold Standard

Progressively shifts selection toward Simplify and Crossover — steering meta-level search toward the gold standard behavioural region (Qi et al., 2023; non-stationary bandits: Branke, 2001)

3

Sustained Late-Stage Improvement

Last Improvement Fraction: 0.3501 vs 0.2677 (Baseline) vs 0.1436 (EoH) — progress continues near the optimum (stagnation avoidance: Rajabi & Witt, 2023)

EoH weakness: Most exploratory profile (Expl% 10.95%, Inten-ratio 0.7761). Its e1/e2 operators prioritise diversity over refinement → persistent exploration bias.
Three behavioural factors drive GA-LLaMEA's advantage: crossover enables trait combination, D-TS steers toward gold standard, and sustained late-stage improvement.
